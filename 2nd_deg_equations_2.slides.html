Second Degree Equations Part 2¶

Factoring¶

Remember that we have seen before that we can factor a quadratic expression in a couple of ways

\begin{align} x^2 - 4 &= (x+2)(x-2) & \text{special product} \\ x^2 + 2x - 24 &= (x-4)(x+6) & \text{factor directly} \end{align}

We have also seen that there are some equations that we cannot factor with direct methods, such as

\begin{equation} x^2 - 3x - 7 \end{equation}

But maybe the quadratic formula can help factor our expression.

Some Review¶

Let's use the quadratic equation on $x^2 - x - 6 = 0$. We get

\begin{align} x &= \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \\ x &= \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{1 - 4(1)(-6)}}{2\cdot 1} \\ x &= \frac{1 \pm\sqrt{1 + 24}}{2} \\ x &= \frac{1 \pm\sqrt{25}}{2} \\ x &= \frac{1 \pm 5}{2} \\ x &= 3, -2 = x_+, x_- \end{align}

Factoring¶

Let us try to factor $$x^2 - x - 6$$ using a new method.

Our goal is to find $c_1$ and $c_2$ such that

\begin{equation} x^2 - x - 6 = (x + c_1)(x + c_2) \end{equation}

The quadratic equation gives us all $x$ such that $x^2 - x - 6 = 0$. This means that we know an $x$ such that

\begin{equation} x^2 - x - 6 = (x + c_1)(x + c_2) = 0 \end{equation}

In order for $(x + c_1)(x+c_2) = 0$, it must be true that eiter $(x + c_1) = 0$ or $(x + c_2) = 0$.

Hmmmmmm.....

Let's try¶

\begin{equation} x_+, x_- = 3, -2 \end{equation}

We want to find $c_1$ and $c_2$ so that $(x+c_1)(x+c_2)=0$ only when $x = x_+$ and $x=x_-$

Idea: let $c_1 = -x_+$ and $c_2 = -x_-$!

We get $x^2 - x - 6 = (x - x_+)(x - x_-)$, which is zero only when $x=x_+$ or $x=x_-$!

This gives us

\begin{equation} x^2 - x - 6 = \left(x - 3 \right)\left(x + 2 \right) \end{equation}

Last Comments on Factoring¶

What happens if $a \neq 1$?

All we have to do is add a factor of $a$ into our factorization!

To factor an expression $ax^2 + bx + c$ for any values of $a, b, c$

Calculate \begin{equation} x_+, x_- = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \end{equation}

Factor $$ax^2 + bx + c = a(x - x_+)(x - x_-)$$

Practice¶

In groups of 3, factor the following expressions with the quadratic formula

\begin{align} x^2 + x - 6 \\ 3x^2 - 9x + 6 \\ 5x^2 + 6x + 1 \end{align}

Practice Example 1¶

$$x^2 + x - 6$$\begin{align} x_+, x_- &= \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{(1)^2 - 4(1)(-6)}}{2(1)} \\ &= \frac{-1 \pm \sqrt{1 + 24}}{2} \\ &= \frac{-1 \pm 5}{2} \\ &= 2, -3 \end{align}\begin{align} x^2 + x - 6 &= (1)(x - x_+)(x - x_-) \\ x^2 + x - 6 &= (x - 2)(x + 3) \end{align}

Practice Example 2¶

$$3x^2 - 9x + 6$$\begin{align} x_+, x_- &= \frac{9 \pm \sqrt{(-9)^2 - 4(3)(6)}}{2(3)} \\ &= \frac{9 \pm \sqrt{81 - 72}}{6} \\ &= \frac{9 \pm \sqrt{9}}{6} \\ &= \frac{9 \pm 3}{6} \\ &= 2, 1 \end{align}\begin{align} 3x^2 - 9x + 6 &= (3)(x - x_+)(x - x_-) \\ 3x^2 - 9x + 6 &= 3(x - 2)(x - 1) \end{align}

Practice Example 3¶

$$5x^2 + 6x + 1$$\begin{align} x_+, x_- &= \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{(6)^2 - 4(5)(1)}}{2(5)} \\ &= \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{36 - 20}}{10} \\ &= \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{16}}{10} \\ &= \frac{-6 \pm 4}{10} \\ &= \frac{-2}{10}, -1 \end{align}\begin{align} 5x^2 + 6x + 1 &= (5)(x - x_+)(x - x_-) \\ 5x^2 + 6x + 1 &= 5\left(x + \frac{2}{10}\right)(x + 1) = (5x + 1)(x + 1) \end{align}

Axis of Symmetry¶

For a parabola given as

\begin{equation} y = ax^2 + bx + c \end{equation}

We can calculalte the $x$ value for the axis of symmetry (the red line from before!) as

\begin{equation} x_{axis} = \frac{-b}{2a} \end{equation}

If we know the equation of the parabola $y = ax^2 + bx + c$ and we calculated the axis of symmetry

\begin{equation} x_{axis} = \frac{-b}{2a} \end{equation}

then the vertex is located at

\begin{equation} (x_{vertex}, y_{vertex}) = \left(\frac{-b}{2a}, \frac{-\left(b^2 - 4ac\right)}{4a} \right) \end{equation}

Practice¶

\begin{equation} (x_{vertex}, y_{vertex}) = \left(\frac{-b}{2a}, \frac{-\left(b^2 - 4ac\right)}{4a} \right) \end{equation}

In groups of 3, find the vertex of each of the following parabolas

\begin{align} x^2 + x - 6 \\ 3x^2 - 9x + 6 \\ 5x^2 + 6x + 1 \\ 6x^2 - 24x - 30 \end{align}

Practice Example 1¶

Parabola: $y = x^2 + x - 6$

\begin{align} x_{vertex} &= \frac{-b}{2a} \\ x_{vertex} &= \frac{-1}{2} = -0.5 \end{align}\begin{align} y_{vertex} &= \frac{-\left(b^2 - 4ac\right)}{4a} \\ y_{vertex} &= \frac{-\left(1^2 - 4(1)(-6)\right)}{4} = \frac{-\left(1 + 24\right)}{4} = \frac{-25}{4} = -6.25 \end{align}\begin{equation} (x_{vertex}, y_{vertex}) = (-0.5, -6.25) \end{equation}

Practice Example 2¶

Parabola: $y = 3x^2 - 9x + 6$

\begin{align} x_{vertex} &= \frac{-b}{2a} \\ x_{vertex} &= \frac{9}{6} = \frac{3}{2} \end{align}\begin{align} y_{vertex} &= \frac{-\left(b^2 - 4ac\right)}{4a} \\ y_{vertex} &= \frac{-\left((-9)^2 - 4(3)(6)\right)}{4(3)} = \frac{-\left(81 - 72\right)}{12} = \frac{-9}{12} = \frac{-3}{4} \end{align}\begin{equation} (x_{vertex}, y_{vertex}) = (3/2, -3/4) \end{equation}

Practice Example 3¶

Parabola: $y = 5x^2 + 6x + 1$

\begin{align} x_{vertex} &= \frac{-b}{2a} \\ x_{vertex} &= \frac{-6}{10} = \frac{-3}{5} \end{align}\begin{align} y_{vertex} &= \frac{-\left(b^2 - 4ac\right)}{4a} \\ y_{vertex} &= \frac{-\left(6^2 - 4(5)(1)\right)}{4(5)} = \frac{-\left(36 - 20\right)}{20} = \frac{-16}{20} = \frac{-4}{5} \end{align}\begin{equation} (x_{vertex}, y_{vertex}) = (-3/5, -4/5) \end{equation}

Practice Example 4¶

Parabola: $y = 6x^2 - 24x - 30$

\begin{align} x_{vertex} &= \frac{-b}{2a} \\ x_{vertex} &= \frac{24}{12} = 2 \end{align}\begin{align} y_{vertex} &= \frac{-\left(b^2 - 4ac\right)}{4a} \\ y_{vertex} &= \frac{-\left(24^2 - 4(6)(-30)\right)}{4(6)} = \frac{-\left(576 + 720\right)}{24} = \frac{-1296}{24} = -54 \end{align}\begin{equation} (x_{vertex}, y_{vertex}) = (2, -54) \end{equation}

We can also use a trick to solve this question. We know that $x_{vertex} = x_{axis}$, so we can just evaluate the parabola at $x_{axis}$ to find $y_{axis}$! This means

\begin{align} y_{vertex} &= 6(x_{vertex})^2 - 24(x_{vertex}) - 30 \\ &= 6(2)^2 - 24(2) - 30 \\ &= 6(4) -48 - 30 \\ &= 24 - 48 - 30 \\ &= -54 \end{align}
